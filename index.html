David Stroud

Product Manager & AI explorer of non-obvious paths to human-level intelligence

I build tools for threat analysts, helping them identify fraudsters and bad actors in massive datasets. My work sits at the intersection of threat intelligence, machine learning, and human judgment.

Humans prove that broad, adaptable intelligence is possible. To nudge machines closer, I’m exploring three novel paths of interest to me: using evolutionary algorithms to grow flexible “mental maps,” probabilistic, data-savvy techniques that squeeze insight from sparse examples and stays robust out of sample, and neurosymbolic systems that blend quick pattern-spotting with slower, rule-based reasoning so every answer carries a built-in why.

When I'm not working, you'll find me cycling, reading across history, philosophy, statistics, and science fiction, or exploring ideas that sit at the crossroads of big questions and real-world impact.

Tacking Through Life

My life hasn't followed a straight line. It's been shaped incrementally—driven by relentless curiosity, a pull toward hard challenges, a search for purpose, and a sense of duty to the people around me.

I grew up in 1990s Silicon Valley, where my time was split between sports, part-time jobs, and racking up long-distance phone charges connecting to early online communities and bulletin board systems.

During my junior year of high school, I ran into an Army recruiter at a career fair. Uninspired by the options in front of me—but drawn to the challenge and purpose the Army offered—I enlisted for four years as a telecommunications specialist.

Before satellites blanketed the sky, high-bandwidth tactical data had to be transmitted via UHF and VHF line-of-sight relays. My job was to set up and maintain the equipment that kept the 'shot'—the signal—alive, connecting the radar air picture of Patriot Missile Batteries across the battlefield.

The Power of Yes

I deployed to Saudi Arabia, where I put my training to the test and experienced a culture completely different from the Bay Area. As my enlistment neared its end, I found I'd grown to enjoy the Army—but felt ready, and finally mature enough, for something more.

One night after returning from Saudi Arabia, I found myself questioning what came next. I'd grown during my enlistment, but I felt ready for greater challenges and responsibility. That's when I caught a Discovery Channel special on a place called West Point. It looked tough and connected to the Army, so it caught my interest.

The next day I asked my Platoon Leader about it—he laughed and asked if I wanted to go. I said yes, and that simple question and my answer set in motion the long road through prep school and, eventually, to West Point.

West Point had its contradictions—tradition and progress, inspiration and frustration—but it pushed me to grow stronger, more resilient, and more decisive in taking control of my circumstances. With the Iraq and Afghanistan wars active, I chose the Infantry to be at the front. I went through Officer Basic Course and Ranger School, then reported to Fort Lewis, my first duty station.

Drawn to Impactful Work

My orders had me heading to a Stryker Brigade bound for relocation to Germany—but I (unnecessarily) worried the war would pass me by. So I convinced a young lieutenant in the Corps personnel office to swap my assignment to the next deploying unit. The Army may be a bureaucratic machine, but I relearned a simple truth: where there's a will, there's a way.

I got what I asked for—and more. Fifteen months in Baghdad and Baqubah challenged everything I thought I knew about courage, complacency, duty, and life. Our mission was to win hearts and minds, but doing so from armored vehicles, rifles in hand, felt oddly ironic. I took it seriously, and we slowly made progress, but I felt a growing need to contribute in a more immediate and tangible way.

One day, I crossed paths with a Special Operations unit getting ready to leave the base. Their quiet confidence, clear mission focus, and sense of urgency stood out. As I learned more about their work, it became clear—they were trained and organized for action and impact. That encounter planted a seed I couldn't ignore leading me in and out of Army Special Operations for over a decade.

Insight is the Multiplier

Before stepping permanently into the Special Operations community, I led an Infantry company in Khost, Afghanistan. Here IEDs shaped everything—hidden, destructive, and omnipresent. Their threat wore on us until we stopped reacting and started looking for patterns. This began by digging into our intelligence.

One evening, we started connecting the dots between fingerprint evidence collected from bomb sites and intelligence from Special Operations night raids. Using basic data analysis, we uncovered something obvious in hindsight: the people planting these bombs lived nearby. It made sense—IEDs were heavy, and most people didn't have cars. That one insight shifted how we mitigated the threat, kept us safer, and gave us a more proactive footing.

Years later, that early experience stayed with me. Even as I spent another decade in Special Operations, rotating through war zones, I held on to the realization that data could drive real-world impact.

Today, I work in threat intelligence, a field overflowing with data where spotting bad actors can feel like hunting a needle in a stack of needles. My curiosity now pulls me toward AI's non-obvious routes to human-level intelligence—letting algorithms evolve their own flexible representations, applying probabilistic reasoning to glean insight from scant data, and crafting neuro-symbolic blends that fuse intuition with logic—because the next leap is unlikely to follow the beaten path.
